How much protein do you actually need?

By F. Perry Wilson, MD MSCE

Protein is everywhere right now — in your water, your popcorn, your coffee creamer. Walk down the snack aisle at Costco and the word "protein" is stamped on practically everything. So it's worth asking: is any of this actually necessary, or are we just in the latest macronutrient craze?

What protein actually is — and why it's different from carbs and fat

We talk about the three macronutrients — carbohydrates, fats, and protein — as if they're roughly equivalent. They're not. Carbohydrates and fats are both primarily energy sources. When your body breaks them down, the byproducts are water and carbon dioxide. You breathe them out. Simple.

Protein is far more complicated. It's made up of amino acids — 20 common ones — that can be assembled in virtually any combination to build the structures of your body: muscle, skin, hair, enzymes, hormones. When you break protein down, you don't just get water and CO₂. You also get ammonia, which is highly toxic and has to be processed and excreted by the kidneys as urea (yes, that's where "urine" gets its name).

Nine of those 20 amino acids are essential — meaning your body cannot synthesize them on its own. You have to get them from food. If you don't, over time, you will develop a serious deficiency. This is fundamentally different from carbohydrates and fats, where the source matters much less than the total amount. With protein, you actually need the specific building blocks. In that sense, protein functions more like a vitamin than a fuel.

The good news: as long as you're eating a reasonable variety of protein-containing foods and hitting an adequate total amount, you'll almost certainly cover all nine essential amino acids. This doesn't require obsessive planning.

How much protein do you need — and what does the evidence say about going higher?

The government's Recommended Daily Allowance (RDA) for protein is 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight — roughly a third of a gram per pound. For a 180-pound person, that's about 65 grams per day. That sounds like a lot until you realize a single chicken breast contains about 30 grams and a cup of Greek yogurt has around 12. Most Americans already hit this target without trying.

But the RDA is designed as a floor — a minimum to prevent deficiency in an institutional population. It was never meant to be a target for people who are active, aging, or trying to build or maintain muscle. In those contexts, a higher intake is probably warranted.

A number that comes up frequently in the research literature is 1.6 grams per kilogram per day — roughly 0.7 grams per pound. For a 180-pound person, that's around 120 grams per day. Some fitness enthusiasts push for a full gram per pound of body weight, but that's on the higher end and requires real dietary effort to hit without also dramatically increasing total calories.

A useful rule of thumb for identifying high-protein foods: multiply the grams of protein in a serving by ten. If that number exceeds the calories in the serving, it qualifies as genuinely protein-dense. Many popular "protein" snack products don't actually clear this bar.

When more protein clearly helps: muscle, aging, and weight loss

The evidence for higher protein intake is strongest in two scenarios: building or maintaining muscle mass, and modestly supporting weight loss.

For muscle, the key point is that protein alone doesn't do it — you have to pair it with resistance training. When you eat protein, its amino acids face a fork in the road: they either get incorporated into new muscle fibers, or they get broken down further into sugar for energy. Your body will happily take the second path if you're not sending a signal through exercise that muscle is actually needed. The protein is the raw material; the workout is the blueprint.

Timing, despite what gym culture suggests, doesn't matter much. Whether you get your protein before or after a workout, all at once or spread across the day, the data in athletic populations is pretty consistent: total daily intake is what drives results, not the timing.

One amino acid worth knowing about is leucine. Unlike its counterparts, leucine appears to have a direct signaling effect on muscle tissue — it may be the molecule muscle uses to "read" the body's nutritional status and decide whether to grow. You'll find leucine in any protein-rich food (chicken, beans, cottage cheese), but leucine supplements are also emerging, with mixed results so far in the research.

For older adults, adequate protein intake takes on additional urgency. A randomized trial called the Pro Muscle Trial enrolled 65 frail elderly participants and gave half of them 30 grams per day of milk protein supplementation alongside resistance training. After a few months, the protein group gained an additional 1.3 kilograms of lean body mass and showed measurable improvement on physical performance tests. The cognitive effects were minimal — but there's a reasonable indirect argument: if maintaining muscle allows older adults to stay active and engaged, that physical capacity may itself support cognitive health over time.

For weight loss, the evidence is more modest. Protein is more satiating than carbohydrates or fat, and some calorie-controlled trials do show a small advantage for higher-protein diets — one 2010 study in men with diabetes found about 3 kilograms of additional weight loss over four months on a 35% protein diet versus 15%, with calories held equal. But these effects are real-but-modest. The more important practical point: high-protein diets tend to work partly because they impose structure and make it harder to eat mindlessly. That's a legitimate mechanism, not magic.

One increasingly relevant scenario: people on GLP-1 medications. These drugs suppress appetite dramatically, which is the goal — but if you're eating significantly less overall, you risk losing muscle alongside fat. Prioritizing protein (while letting calories from carbohydrates and fat fall) helps preserve lean mass during GLP-1-driven weight loss.

The risks of very high protein intake

Yes, you can have too much protein — though the bar is higher than most people will realistically reach.

The most established risk involves kidney function. Protein metabolism generates ammonia, which the kidneys must filter and excrete. A high-protein diet measurably increases the kidneys' filtration rate — from a typical 100 milliliters per minute up to 110 or 120. For healthy kidneys, the short-term significance of this is unclear. But for people with underlying kidney disease, the data is compelling: chronically pushing the kidneys to run above capacity accelerates the loss of kidney function over time. I no longer routinely tell patients with kidney disease to severely restrict protein (because muscle preservation matters too), but I certainly don't encourage supplementation in that population.

A more speculative concern involves the amino acid leucine and its activation of a molecule called mTOR (mammalian target of rapamycin). mTOR promotes growth — in muscle, that's the point. But mTOR is also a known cancer-promoting signal, and chronically elevated mTOR activity is a step in the direction of unchecked cellular growth. There are many steps between "slightly elevated mTOR" and cancer, so I wouldn't overweight this risk. But it's worth acknowledging that growth signaling, broadly speaking, isn't always something you want to maximize.

Bottom line

Most people eating a typical American diet are already meeting the minimum protein threshold. The question is whether you have a reason to go higher — and for many people, there is one. If you're doing resistance training, trying to maintain muscle mass as you age, managing your weight, or on a GLP-1 medication, targeting something closer to 1.6 grams per kilogram per day (roughly 0.7 grams per pound) is well-supported by evidence. Getting there likely requires some intentional planning and, for higher targets, protein supplementation like whey powder.

People with kidney disease should talk to their doctor before significantly increasing protein intake. And for everyone else: prioritize whole-food protein sources when possible — particularly plant-based ones — to avoid inadvertently gutting your fiber intake in the process.

Protein deserves its moment in the spotlight, just not the unhinged version of it.
